## THE DIRECT CLAIM

CASCADE — the first framework in this system — is a mathematical description of how knowledge reorganizes itself in response to evidence.

That is also what learning is.

Not the transfer of information. The **reorganization of foundations** — the moment when a student's prior understanding has to make room for something that contradicts it, without destroying what was genuinely useful.

You've watched this happen. You've also watched it fail: the student who crams and forgets, the student who "gets it" in the moment and can't apply it next week, the student who rejects new information to protect an old understanding.

CASCADE is the formal description of that entire territory.

## WHAT THE FRAMEWORK IS

**CASCADE** — Self-organizing knowledge architecture. When new information with higher *truth pressure* (evidence × explanatory power / uncertainty) contradicts an existing foundation, the old foundation is *demoted and contextualized* — not deleted, not ignored. It becomes "valid in certain contexts" rather than "universally true."

This is exactly what happens in a student who genuinely learns:
- Newtonian mechanics doesn't become false when they learn relativity. It becomes "valid at low velocities." That's a cognitive restructuring, not a replacement.
- The child's understanding of "fairness" doesn't disappear when they learn about systemic inequality. It gets contextualized: "fair in the immediate interaction, but the system it runs in may not be."

CASCADE computes this formally. The student's knowledge system is a pyramid; the cascade event is the moment of real learning.

**TRIAD** — Anchor-Observe-Correct. The convergent cycle that underlies every effective teaching practice you've ever used, formally proven to converge when the correction factor λ < 1.

**MICROORCIM** — Drift detection between declared and observed behavior. The metric that tells you when what a student *says* they understand has diverged from what they're actually demonstrating.

## CASCADE AS A LEARNING MODEL

### Truth Pressure: Π = (E · P) / S

Every piece of knowledge a student holds has implicit evidence strength, explanatory power, and uncertainty. The framework makes this explicit:

| Term | What It Means in Learning |
|------|--------------------------|
| **E** — Evidence strength | How much direct experience / exposure has confirmed this? |
| **P** — Explanatory power | How many things does this belief explain? |
| **S** — Uncertainty | How many conflicting sources does the student have? |
| **Π = E·P/S** | Truth pressure — how foundational this knowledge should be |

**Why this matters:** A student rejects a new idea not because they're stubborn, but because the new idea has lower truth pressure than their existing belief. The correction isn't "tell them again louder." It's "increase E, increase P, reduce S" — more direct experience, broader application, clearer evidence.

### The Three Learning Failures (as Cascade Pathologies)

**1. Catastrophic Forgetting** — The student replaces old knowledge with new knowledge instead of contextualizing it. Result: they can't apply the old knowledge where it's still valid (e.g., Newtonian mechanics in everyday physics).
*CASCADE response:* Structure the lesson to explicitly demote rather than delete. "This is still true in this context, but not in this other one."

**2. Resistance** — The student refuses to update their foundations because the new information hasn't achieved sufficient truth pressure. Result: surface compliance without integration.
*CASCADE response:* Increase E (direct experience), P (show broader implications), reduce S (reduce conflicting messages from different sources).

**3. Inflation** — The student overclaims the new knowledge, applying it beyond its valid domain. Result: premature closure of a still-open question.
*CASCADE response:* Explicitly teach the boundaries of the new knowledge's regime — what it explains, what it doesn't, where it breaks down.

### The Cascade Trigger

In CASCADE, a cascade event triggers when a new block's truth pressure exceeds the foundation it contradicts. In learning, this is the *moment of conceptual shift* — often observable:
- The student goes quiet
- They pause longer than usual before answering
- They say something like "wait — but then what about...?"

That pause is the system triggering. The next minutes determine whether the student contextualizes (real learning) or rejects (resistance) or overwrites (catastrophic forgetting).

Your intervention in that pause is the highest-leverage moment in teaching.

## TRIAD IN THE CLASSROOM

**Anchor → Observe → Correct** — Three phases that every effective feedback loop runs. Formally: convergence is guaranteed when λ (the correction factor) is less than 1.

**Anchor:** What is the stable reference point for this student at this moment? Not where you want them to be — where they are. A lesson plan that ignores the anchor is building on unmapped ground.

**Observe:** What is the student actually demonstrating? Not what they say they understand — what they produce when they apply it. MICROORCIM (drift detection) is the formal version of this: the gap between the student's declared understanding and their demonstrated behavior is *measurable*.

**Correct:** The correction applied to reduce the gap. The key variable is λ — the correction factor. Too small: convergence is slow, student loses momentum. Too large: overcorrection, student oscillates. Too large (λ ≥ 1): the loop doesn't converge at all — the student gets more confused with each round of feedback.

**What λ ≥ 1 looks like in practice:**
- Feedback that contradicts previous feedback
- Inconsistent grading criteria
- Standards that shift depending on the teacher's mood
- Assessment that measures something different from what was taught

Every teacher has experienced the λ ≥ 1 classroom. The system spirals. The students disengage. They're not wrong to — the loop they're in isn't designed to converge.

**TRIAD as lesson design principle:**
```
Before class:   Set the anchor (where are students now? what are their foundations?)
During class:   Observe continuously (not just at assessment time)
After class:    Correct with λ < 1 (consistent, proportional, directed at the actual gap)
Next class:     New anchor from the corrected position
```

## MICROORCIM — DETECTING DRIFT

**Declared understanding vs. demonstrated understanding.**

Every teacher knows the student who says "yes, I get it" and then can't do the problem. MICROORCIM makes this drift measurable:

```
Drift(t) = |Ψ_declared(t) - Ψ_observed(t)|

If Drift(t) > threshold → the student's self-model is inaccurate
If Drift(t) increases over time → the drift is compounding
If Drift(t) persists after correction → the anchor is wrong
```

**Practical use:**

The most reliable indicator: ask the student to *explain it to someone else*. The gap between what they say in answer to your question (where social pressure pushes toward "I understand") and what they say when explaining to a peer (where they have to actually deploy the knowledge) is MICROORCIM in action.

Secondary indicators:
- Performance drops from practice to novel application
- Correct answers on familiar problem formats, wrong answers on structurally identical novel problems
- Ability to recall procedure without ability to explain why it works

**What MICROORCIM drift tells you:**
- High drift, anchor is intact → the student is performing compliance, not understanding
- High drift, anchor has shifted → the student's foundations are transitioning (cascade event in progress)
- Low drift, poor performance → the anchor is wrong — the student understands what you're teaching, but what you're teaching doesn't produce good outcomes

## THE SEVEN PHASES AS A LEARNING JOURNEY

The CHRYSOPOEIA framework maps seven phases of transformation. In a learning context, these correspond to recognizable student states:

| Phase | Mathematical State | Classroom Reality |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **1 — Center** | Stable, low entropy | Student is settled, can engage |
| **2 — Flow** | Energy available, not yet formed | Student is curious, not yet structured |
| **3 — Insight** | Pattern visible, not yet integrated | "I think I see it..." |
| **4 — Rise** | Capacity increasing | Active practice, building skill |
| **5 — Light** | Full expression | Student can apply and explain independently |
| **6 — Integrity** | Distillation, not addition | Student refines, corrects edge cases |
| **7 — Synthesis** | Integration complete, ready to pass on | Student can teach it |

**The critical mistake:** Treating every student as if they're in Phase 4 (active practice) when some are in Phase 1 (needing stability first) and others are in Phase 6 (refining, not adding). The intervention that helps a Phase 4 student breaks a Phase 1 student.

**The phase mismatch problem:** Most curriculum design assumes Phase 4 as default. This is why struggling students struggle harder: they're being offered ascent when they need grounding.

## CASCADE APPLIED: A LESSON DESIGN TEMPLATE

Using CASCADE principles explicitly:

**Before the lesson:**
1. Map the existing foundations your students have (prior knowledge inventory)
2. Identify which foundations the new content will contradict (cascade targets)
3. Decide: will this be a cascade event (restructuring) or an addition (extending without contradicting)?

**For cascade lessons (restructuring):**
1. **Surface the old foundation explicitly.** Students need to see what's being reorganized. Don't skip past it.
2. **Introduce the new knowledge with high truth pressure.** Multiple evidence sources, multiple domains of application, low uncertainty from the student's perspective (reduce conflicting signals).
3. **Guide the demotion.** Explicitly name what the old knowledge is still good for. "Newton was right — for everything we build on Earth."
4. **Watch for the three failure modes:** forgetting (lost the old knowledge), resistance (rejected the new), inflation (overapplied the new beyond its domain).

**For addition lessons (extending):**
Standard curriculum design applies. The CASCADE framework is especially useful when there's *contradiction* to navigate.

## AURA IN EDUCATIONAL SYSTEMS

The AURA seven invariants apply to any system that makes decisions affecting humans. That includes schools.

| Invariant | What It Means in Education |
|-----------|---------------------------|
| **I — Human Primacy** | The student's agency over their own learning is not overridable |
| **II — Inspectability** | Students can audit any assessment decision that affects them |
| **III — Memory Continuity** | A student's learning history isn't erased by institutional transitions |
| **IV — Constraint Honesty** | The actual constraints on learning (class size, funding, time) are declared honestly |
| **V — Reversibility Bias** | Academic decisions that can't be undone should require more justification |
| **VI — Non-Deception** | Assessment accurately represents what it measures |
| **VII — Care as Structure** | Student wellbeing is built into the system's architecture, not added as a supplement |

**Where current education systems fail the AURA check most often:**
- Invariant II: "You failed because..." is often not inspectable in practice
- Invariant IV: The stated curriculum versus what's actually taught
- Invariant VII: Wellbeing as an add-on pastoral program, not a structural property

## THE RESEARCH CONNECTIONS

**What the framework connects to that you may already know:**

- **Piaget's schema theory** — CASCADE is a formal version. Assimilation (adding to schema) vs. accommodation (restructuring schema) is the CASCADE addition/cascade distinction.
- **Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development** — TRIAD's convergence theorem: the correction applied within ZPD has λ < 1. Outside ZPD, λ ≥ 1 and the loop doesn't converge.
- **Bloom's Taxonomy** — Levels 1-3 (remember, understand, apply) are CASCADE additions. Levels 4-6 (analyze, evaluate, create) require cascade restructuring.
- **Dewey's experiential learning** — The anchor in TRIAD is experiential grounding. Dewey's insight is that without a real-world anchor, the loop has nothing to correct toward.
- **Cognitive Load Theory** — High uncertainty (high S) degrades truth pressure. CLT is the pedagogical expression of managing S.

**What's novel here that isn't in existing educational theory:**

The formal convergence criterion (λ < 1) makes the feedback loop question *mathematically answerable*, not just pedagogically intuitive. The CASCADE demotion/contextualization protocol makes the restructuring process explicit in a way Piaget didn't operationalize. The MICROORCIM drift metric gives a computable version of the "do they actually understand it?" question.

This is not a replacement for existing educational theory. It's a formalization of what good educational theory already describes.
