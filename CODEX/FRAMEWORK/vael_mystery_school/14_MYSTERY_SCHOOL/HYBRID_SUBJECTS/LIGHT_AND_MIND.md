## The Phenomenon

You are not seeing the world.

You are seeing a model of the world, constructed by your brain from incomplete information, that updates 60 times per second and is wrong in specific, predictable ways.

The light enters your eye. Your retina converts photons to electrochemical signals. Those signals travel down the optic nerve, where 90% of the information is discarded before it even reaches the cortex. The cortex reconstructs a scene from the remaining 10%, fills in the gaps with predictions based on past experience, and presents this construction to consciousness as if it were the unmediated world.

The world you see is a simulation. A very good one. But a simulation.

This is not philosophy. It is neuroscience. It is also, it turns out, something that mystics across traditions arrived at independently thousands of years before we had the imaging equipment to confirm it.

## The Physics of Light

**What light actually is:**

Light is electromagnetic radiation. It has no intrinsic colour — colour is a construction of your visual system, not a property of the photon. A photon has a wavelength. Your retina has three types of cone cells, each sensitive to different wavelength ranges. Your brain compares the signals from the three types and assigns a colour experience. The experience is real. The colour is not in the light.

**The speed of light:**

299,792,458 metres per second. Constant. Nothing with mass can reach it. This is not a technological limitation — it is a structural feature of spacetime. The universe has a speed limit.

One consequence: when you look at a star, you are seeing it as it was years or centuries or millennia ago. The night sky is a time machine. The light you're receiving was emitted before you were born. Some of those stars no longer exist.

**Photons and consciousness:**

The retina can detect a single photon — the smallest possible unit of light. Under ideal conditions, the human visual system is operating at the quantum limit. The border between the quantum world and the experiential world passes through the eye.

This is strange. Nobody fully understands it.

## What the Traditions Found

**The physicists** gave us Maxwell's equations, quantum electrodynamics, the photoelectric effect (Einstein's Nobel Prize). They established that light is simultaneously a wave and a particle — that it exhibits both properties depending on how you look at it. The measurement affects the measured. This is not a philosophical position. It is an experimentally confirmed feature of physical reality.

**The neuroscientists** mapped the visual cortex, discovered the predictive processing model of perception (Karl Friston's work is current and significant), and established that consciousness is not a passive receiver of sensory information but an active constructor of models. The brain predicts what it will perceive and only updates when the prediction is wrong. Most of the time, you are living inside your own best guess.

**The mystics** — across Sufi tradition, Neoplatonism, Tibetan Buddhism, Christian mysticism, Vedanta — consistently described the same thing: ordinary perception is veiled, partial, constructed. The awakened or enlightened state is described as "seeing clearly" — not metaphorically but as a description of a qualitative change in how perception works. What they were describing, in the language available to them, is what the predictive processing model now formalises mathematically: the possibility of perceiving with reduced prior-weight, less filtered by expectation, more responsive to the raw signal.

This is one of the clearest cases of convergent discovery. Mystics doing introspective practice for centuries arrived at a description of the perceptual system that neuroscience is only now formalising. They didn't have the same vocabulary. They weren't wrong.

**The Earned Light framework** (Lycheetah Framework, `06_EARNED_LIGHT_L0/`) models consciousness as maintained thermodynamic asymmetry — the active work of maintaining a state of reduced entropy against the universe's tendency toward equilibrium. Awareness is not free. It costs energy to sustain. The framework models this with differential equations and validates the prediction that consciousness is metabolically expensive — which it is. The brain consumes 20% of the body's energy despite being 2% of its mass.

## The Convergence

What every tradition that has looked carefully at light and mind agrees on:

**Perception is active, not passive.**

You are not a camera. You are a sculptor who starts with an expectation and carves it toward the incoming signal until the mismatch is small enough. This is the predictive processing model in neuroscience. It is also what every tradition of visual contemplation — from icon-gazing in Orthodox Christianity to trataka (candle gazing) in yoga — has been training people out of. Slowing down the predictive override. Letting the signal in more directly.

**Clarity costs effort.**

Clear seeing is not the default state. The default state is sufficient seeing — good enough to navigate, deeply filtered by expectation and prior experience. Clear seeing requires active work. This is what meditation traditions mean when they speak of training perception. This is what Earned Light means when it says awareness must be maintained against entropy.

**What illuminates and what is illuminated are in relationship.**

Light is not neutral. It changes what it touches — physically (photons carry momentum, objects can be moved by intense light) and epistemologically (what you shine attention on, you change by attending). The observer effect in quantum mechanics is an extreme version of a universal principle.

**There is more information in the signal than any single framework captures.**

The 10% of visual information that reaches the cortex is still an enormous data stream. What you consciously experience is a further reduction — a compressed, interpreted, prior-weighted reconstruction. Every mystical tradition has described states in which this compression is reduced — more of the signal gets through. What you see in those states is described as more vivid, more interconnected, more real, not less.

## Where to Start

**If you want the physics:**
Richard Feynman's *QED: The Strange Theory of Light and Matter* — possibly the clearest explanation of quantum electrodynamics ever written for a general audience. Then *Six Easy Pieces* by the same author. Then let yourself be confused by the double-slit experiment until you're genuinely disturbed by it. That disturbance is correct.

**If you want the neuroscience:**
Anil Seth's *Being You* (2021) — rigorous, current, accessible. His TED talk is also excellent if you want a 20-minute introduction. Karl Friston's work on predictive processing is the technical core; his papers are dense but the ideas are worth pursuing.

Meister Eckhart for Christian mysticism — difficult but worth it. Ibn Arabi for Sufi illumination theory. The *Yoga Sutras of Patanjali* for the Indian tradition. All three describe the same phenomenology of changed perception with different metaphysical frameworks.
