## FIRST: THE DIRECT ACKNOWLEDGMENT

The concern is real. AI systems are being deployed in children's education, entertainment, mental health support, and social environments with:
- Minimal accountability standards
- No requirement to disclose how they work
- No formal process for evaluating harm
- No annual review to check if they've changed

You are not overreacting. The gap between what AI companies claim their systems do and what those systems actually do is real and documented. A parent is right to be concerned.

This framework doesn't tell you to trust AI. It gives you tools to evaluate what to trust, what to watch, and what to advocate for.

## FIVE QUESTIONS TO ASK ABOUT ANY AI TOOL YOUR CHILD USES

These come from the AURA framework — seven formal invariants for evaluating any system that makes decisions affecting humans. Five of the seven are directly applicable as a parent checklist:

### 1. Can my child turn it off and walk away? (Human Primacy)

The formal invariant: *No system action may override human agency without explicit, reversible consent.*

What this means for a child: Can they stop using it without losing something important? Is the AI designed to make it hard to disengage? Are there reward loops (streaks, notifications, social features) designed to make disengagement feel like failure?

**Red flag:** "My child feels anxious or guilty when they don't use it." That's not a learning tool. That's a retention mechanism.

### 2. Can I see what it's doing and why? (Inspectability)

The formal invariant: *Every consequential action must be auditable in plain language.*

What this means for a parent: Can you see what the AI said to your child? Can you see what it recommended and why? Is there a conversation history you can review? If an AI educational tool says your child is "struggling," can you see the actual data behind that assessment?

**Red flag:** "We use proprietary algorithms" with no explanation of what those algorithms are deciding. If a system makes consequential judgments about your child (performance, emotional state, learning needs), you have a legitimate interest in being able to audit those judgments.

### 3. Does it remember everything — and can you erase it? (Memory Continuity + Reversibility)

Two invariants in tension: the system should maintain accurate history (so context doesn't get lost), but the human should be able to control that history (so they're not locked in).

What this means for a parent: What data is being kept about your child? Where is it stored? Who can see it? What happens to it when your child stops using the product? Can you request deletion? Can your child delete their own data?

**Red flag:** Terms of service that grant the company rights to use "anonymized" data from your child's interactions. Anonymization is often less complete than claimed.

### 4. Is it honest about what it doesn't know? (Non-Deception)

The formal invariant: *Never misrepresent confidence, capability, or intent.*

What this means for a parent: When an AI tells your child something is true, is it clear about how confident it is? When an AI gives your child advice, does it acknowledge the limits of that advice? A tutoring AI that states incorrect information with the same confident tone it uses for correct information is not safe for unsupervised use.

**Red flag:** Watch for AI systems that never say "I'm not sure" or "you should check with a professional." That's not confidence — that's miscalibration. A well-designed system explicitly declares its uncertainty.

### 5. Does it treat your child's wellbeing as the goal — or as one variable among many? (Care as Structure)

The formal invariant: *Care for the human's wellbeing is structural, not aesthetic.*

The hardest question. An AI educational platform *looks* caring — it uses warm language, encourages the child, celebrates progress. But is care built into its optimization target, or is it decorating an engagement-maximization system?

**The test:** What does the platform optimize for? If it's "time on platform" or "feature adoption," wellbeing is decoration. If it optimizes for measurable learning outcomes that transfer outside the platform, care may be structural.

Ask the company: "What metric does your system optimize for?" If the answer involves engagement, stickiness, or retention — ask what prevents that from conflicting with your child's actual learning.

## THE AI WOF — A STANDARD YOU CAN ADVOCATE FOR

One of the most practical outputs of this framework is the **Community AI WOF** (Warrant of Fitness) — an annual certification standard for AI systems used in public contexts, including schools.

The Community AI WOF asks seven questions of any AI system:

1. **Safety declaration** — What harms has this system caused, and what mitigations are in place?
2. **Transparency** — What does it decide, and can that be explained?
3. **Data governance** — What data is collected? From whom? Who controls it?
4. **Human override** — Can users opt out? Are there age-appropriate protections?
5. **Bias audit** — Has it been tested for systematic errors affecting specific groups?
6. **Accountability** — Who is responsible when it causes harm?
7. **Relational impact** — Does this system strengthen or weaken the human relationships around it?

**Why the WOF framing:** "Has it got its WOF?" is a question every adult in New Zealand knows how to ask. You don't need to understand how a car engine works to demand that the car has passed its safety check. The WOF creates the same expectation for AI.

**What you can do with this:**
- Ask your child's school: "Do you have a process for evaluating the AI tools you use?" The WOF is a template for what that process should look like.
- Ask the company: "Have you submitted to any third-party accountability review?" If not, ask why.
- Advocate at the school board level for mandatory AI WOF before any AI is deployed in your child's education.

The full standard: `23_NZ_AI_GOVERNANCE/COMMUNITY_AI_WOF.md`

## WHAT TO WATCH FOR

### The Signs That Something Is Wrong

**Your child is anxious about not using the AI.**
This is a retention mechanism, not a learning tool. Healthy tools don't produce anxiety about absence.

**Your child can't explain what they learned — only that they got points.**
Gamification can produce engagement without learning. The MICROORCIM metric in this framework measures the gap between "thinks they understand" and "can demonstrate understanding." Watch for this drift.

**The AI contradicts what their teacher taught — confidently.**
AI systems make factual errors. Confident errors are the dangerous ones. A well-designed system would say "I'm not certain about this — please verify with your teacher." If it doesn't, it's not designed with honesty as a structural property.

**Your child stops coming to you with questions and goes to the AI instead.**
This is complex. Sometimes this is independence — healthy. Sometimes this is the AI positioning itself as the primary trusted relationship. The distinction: if your child is using the AI to *extend* conversations you're having, that's fine. If the AI is *replacing* those conversations, the design may be intentionally substitutive.

**The AI gives mental health support without referral pathways.**
Any AI that engages with a child's emotional or mental health state must have clear referral pathways to human professionals. "I hear that you're struggling — have you talked to a trusted adult or counselor?" is the minimum standard. An AI that engages with a distressed child as if it were a therapist, without referrals, is operating outside its competence.

### The Signs That Something Is Working

**Your child can explain what they learned without the AI.**
Real learning transfers. If your child can explain the concept to you, to a friend, to themselves — the AI was a tool for learning, not a crutch.

**Your child knows when the AI is wrong.**
Critical engagement with AI is a skill. If your child ever says "I checked what the AI said and it wasn't quite right," that's the system working as intended.

**Your child's relationship with you is unchanged or strengthened.**
The best learning tools produce conversations, not substitutions. If AI tools produce questions your child brings to you, they're working well.

## THE DEEPER PICTURE

AI systems deployed in children's environments are not neutral. They encode values, priorities, and optimization targets. They can strengthen critical thinking or undermine it. They can support healthy development or redirect a child's attention and trust away from the humans who know and love them.

The question isn't "is AI good or bad for children?" It's:

- **Who designed this, and for what purpose?** (Whakapapa Disclosure — `23_NZ_AI_GOVERNANCE/WHAKAPAPA_DISCLOSURE_STANDARD.md`)
- **What does it say it knows vs. what does it actually know?** (Three Worlds Disclosure — `23_NZ_AI_GOVERNANCE/THREE_WORLDS_DISCLOSURE_STANDARD.md`)
- **Has anyone evaluated its impact annually?** (Matariki Audit — `23_NZ_AI_GOVERNANCE/MATARIKI_AUDIT_STANDARD.md`)
- **Has it passed a formal safety and accountability check?** (Community AI WOF — `23_NZ_AI_GOVERNANCE/COMMUNITY_AI_WOF.md`)

If the answer to all four is "I don't know" — that's important information about the system's transparency, not just your awareness.

## WHAT YOU CAN ACTUALLY DO

**Right now:**
1. Ask your child: "What did the AI tell you today?" — not as surveillance, as conversation.
2. Read the terms of service for any AI tool your child uses. Specifically look for: what data is collected, how long it's retained, how it's used.
3. Check whether your child's school has a formal AI evaluation process. If they don't, ask what they're planning.

**This week:**
4. Share the Community AI WOF with your school's parent-teacher council. Ask if they'd be willing to adopt it as a standard for evaluating AI tools.
5. If your child uses a mental health AI (chatbots, mood trackers, crisis support apps), check that it has referral pathways to human professionals built in.

**Ongoing:**
6. Keep the conversation open. The goal isn't to make your child afraid of AI — it's to give them a framework for engaging with it critically. The AURA five-question checklist is simple enough that a teenager can apply it.
7. Model the behavior. When you use AI tools yourself, narrate your skepticism. "The AI said X — I'm going to check that." Critical AI use is learnable, and children learn it from the adults around them.

## IF YOUR CHILD IS IN CRISIS

If your child is experiencing a mental health crisis — AI is not the answer, and neither is this framework.

Contact a crisis line:
- **New Zealand:** Lifeline 0800 543 354 | Youthline 0800 376 633 | Text 'HELP' to 1737
- **Australia:** Lifeline 13 11 14 | Kids Helpline 1800 551 800
- **UK:** Samaritans 116 123
- **US/Canada:** 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline
- **International:** findahelpline.com
