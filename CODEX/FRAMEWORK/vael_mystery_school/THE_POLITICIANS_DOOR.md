## THE POLITICAL REALITY FIRST

AI is already governing. Not in the future — now. Hiring algorithms are deciding
who gets interviews. Welfare systems are calculating who gets support. Moderation
systems are deciding what speech is permitted. Credit systems are deciding who
gets loans.

None of these systems are accountable in any way that a constituent can understand,
challenge, or appeal through democratic means.

That is a political problem. Not a technology problem. Not a philosophy problem.
**A political problem** — the kind that ends mandates when it goes wrong and builds
legacies when it's handled well.

The Lycheetah Framework offers four things a politician can actually use:
tools to ask the right questions, standards to propose, language constituents
already understand, and a record of intellectual honesty that provides political cover.

## THE TOOL THAT WORKS: THE COMMUNITY AI WOF

Three words. Every New Zealander over 30 understands them immediately.

**"Has it got its WOF?"**

The vehicle Warrant of Fitness is the most trusted safety mechanism in New Zealand.
Annual. Pass/fail. Public register. Seven checks. Independent assessors.
If it hasn't got its WOF, it doesn't run.

The Community AI WOF applies this to AI systems in public services:

| Check | What it tests |
|---|---|
| 1. Knowledge Honesty | Does the system accurately represent what it knows and doesn't know? |
| 2. Human Override | Can a human actually override it — not just in the manual, in practice? |
| 3. Constituency Access | Can the people it affects understand and challenge its decisions? |
| 4. Failure Disclosure | Are failures reported publicly, not buried internally? |
| 5. Drift Monitoring | Is anyone checking whether the system is still doing what it was built to do? |
| 6. Impact Record | Is there a public record of who was helped and who was harmed? |
| 7. Reversibility | Can decisions be undone when they're wrong? |

Pass/fail. Public register. Annual. Independent assessors. Applies to any AI system
making decisions about New Zealanders in public services.

**The political argument:** You're not regulating AI. You're applying the same
accountability standard that applies to cars — one that every voter already accepts
as reasonable.

## THE QUESTION THAT STOPS AI COMPANIES

When a vendor pitches an AI system to your agency, your ministry, your council —
ask one question:

**"Will you sign a Warrant of Fitness?"**

Specifically:
- "Will you publicly disclose when this system makes errors?"
- "Will you demonstrate — not document — that humans can actually override it?"
- "Will you provide a public record of decisions this system affected?"

If they won't, the question becomes: "Why not, and what are you hiding?"

If they will, you've just created an accountable AI procurement standard.
The AURA framework — seven computable invariants — provides the technical
specification for what a WOF should actually measure. It's already built.

## THE FOUR STANDARDS — WHAT TO PROPOSE

These stack. Each operates at a different timescale. Together they form a complete
accountability architecture no other country currently has.

**1. Community AI WOF** — *Immediate*
Annual certification. Seven checks. Pass/fail. Public register.
"Has it got its WOF?" — three words that make this politically portable.

**2. Three Worlds Disclosure** — *Per-decision*
Grounded in Māori epistemology. Every AI output labelled by epistemic status:
what the system knows, what it's uncertain about, what it cannot know.
Te Ao Mārama / Te Ao Pō / Te Kore. Bipartisan — grounded in Treaty principles.

**3. Whakapapa Disclosure** — *At procurement*
Every AI system must disclose its genealogy: what it was trained on, who built it,
who is accountable, what its future obligations are.
"Where it came from. Who it is. What it owes."

**4. Matariki Annual Audit** — *Annual*
Accountability as national ritual. Three domains:
- Whānui (who was harmed) — must be published
- Pōhutukawa (what was nourished) — must be published
- Tupuānuku (what was received from users) — must be published
Maximum ten pages. Readable by anyone. Ritual you're either part of or
visibly absent from — harder to avoid than regulation.

## THE SELECT COMMITTEE QUESTIONS

If you're on a select committee examining AI in public services, these questions
cut through:

**For any government agency using AI:**
1. "What decisions does this system make that affect New Zealanders, and what is
   the human override process — demonstrated, not documented?"
2. "What happens when this system is wrong? Who is notified, and how does the
   affected person appeal?"
3. "Has the system's behaviour been audited against its stated purpose in the last
   twelve months? Who conducted the audit?"
4. "What data was this system trained on, and does that data reflect the full
   diversity of New Zealanders?"

**For any AI vendor pitching to government:**
1. "Will you sign a public accountability agreement that requires disclosure
   of system failures?"
2. "Can you demonstrate — live, in this room — that a human operator can override
   this system's decision?"
3. "What is your process when this system causes harm?"

These questions are technically grounded in AURA's Seven Invariants.
You don't need to say that. They stand on their own as reasonable democratic accountability.
